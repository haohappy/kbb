Statins: Mechanism of Action and Clinical Use

Statins are the most widely prescribed class of cholesterol-lowering drugs. They work by inhibiting HMG-CoA reductase, the rate-limiting enzyme in the mevalonate pathway of cholesterol synthesis.

Common Statins:
- Atorvastatin (Lipitor) - High potency, long half-life
- Rosuvastatin (Crestor) - Highest potency statin
- Simvastatin (Zocor) - Moderate potency, prodrug
- Pravastatin (Pravachol) - Hydrophilic, fewer drug interactions
- Lovastatin (Mevacor) - First approved statin, derived from fungi
- Fluvastatin (Lescol) - Lowest potency
- Pitavastatin (Livalo) - Minimal CYP450 metabolism

Mechanism:
1. Statins competitively inhibit HMG-CoA reductase
2. This reduces intracellular cholesterol synthesis in the liver
3. The liver upregulates LDL receptors on its surface
4. More LDL-cholesterol is cleared from the bloodstream
5. Net effect: 20-60% reduction in LDL-cholesterol

Pleiotropic Effects:
- Anti-inflammatory (reduce CRP)
- Endothelial function improvement
- Plaque stabilization
- Antithrombotic effects
- Reduced oxidative stress

Side Effects:
- Myalgia (muscle pain) - 5-10% of patients
- Rhabdomyolysis (rare but serious)
- Hepatotoxicity (rare)
- New-onset diabetes (slight increase in risk)
- Cognitive effects (controversial)

Clinical Evidence:
The landmark 4S trial (1994) demonstrated 30% reduction in all-cause mortality with simvastatin.
JUPITER trial showed benefit of rosuvastatin even in patients with normal LDL but elevated CRP.
