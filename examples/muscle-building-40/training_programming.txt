Resistance Training Programming for Men Over 40

The fundamental principles of hypertrophy remain the same regardless of age: progressive overload, sufficient volume, adequate recovery. However, the application must be modified for the 40+ demographic.

Training Frequency:
- 3-4 days per week is optimal (vs. 5-6 for younger trainees)
- Full-body or upper/lower splits work best due to longer recovery needs
- Push/Pull/Legs can work but may require more rest days between sessions
- Minimum 48 hours between training the same muscle group (72 hours preferred for men over 45)

Recommended Split Options:

Option 1: Full Body (3 days/week)
Monday: Full Body A
Wednesday: Full Body B
Friday: Full Body C
Best for beginners or those returning to training after a break

Option 2: Upper/Lower (4 days/week)
Monday: Upper Body
Tuesday: Lower Body
Thursday: Upper Body
Friday: Lower Body
Best balance of volume and recovery for intermediate lifters

Option 3: Push/Pull/Legs (3-6 days/week)
Can be run 3 days (one rotation per week) or 6 days (two rotations)
For advanced trainees with good recovery capacity

Volume Guidelines:
- 10-20 sets per muscle group per week (start at the lower end)
- 8-15 reps per set for hypertrophy (sweet spot for older lifters)
- Heavy singles and triples (<5 reps) should be used sparingly due to joint stress
- RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) 7-8 out of 10 for most sets — avoid consistent training to failure
- Deload every 4-6 weeks (reduce volume by 40-50%)

Exercise Selection (Joint-Friendly Priorities):

Compound Movements (Core of the Program):
1. Squat variations: Goblet squat, front squat, safety bar squat preferred over low-bar back squat (less shoulder and low back stress)
2. Hinge movements: Trap bar deadlift, Romanian deadlift, hip thrust (conventional deadlift places more stress on lower back)
3. Horizontal press: Dumbbell bench press, push-ups (barbell bench press with a close grip if shoulders allow)
4. Vertical press: Landmine press, seated dumbbell press (overhead pressing may aggravate shoulder impingement)
5. Rows: Cable rows, chest-supported rows, single-arm dumbbell rows
6. Pull-ups/Lat pulldowns: Neutral grip preferred

Isolation Movements (Supporting):
- Face pulls and band pull-aparts for shoulder health (every session)
- Bicep curls, tricep pushdowns for arm development
- Lateral raises for shoulder width
- Leg curls, leg extensions for quadricep/hamstring balance
- Calf raises (2-3x per week, 15-20 reps)

Warm-Up Protocol (Non-Negotiable for 40+):
10-15 minutes minimum:
1. 5 minutes light cardio (rowing machine ideal — engages full body)
2. Dynamic stretching: leg swings, arm circles, hip circles, cat-cow
3. Band work: pull-aparts, dislocations, monster walks
4. Progressive warm-up sets: 50% x 8, 70% x 5, 85% x 3 before working sets

Recovery Considerations:
- Sleep 7-9 hours (non-negotiable — testosterone and GH are primarily released during deep sleep)
- Stress management: elevated cortisol directly antagonizes muscle building
- Consider reducing overall training volume when work/life stress is high
- Active recovery: walking 8,000-10,000 steps daily, light yoga or stretching on off days
- Foam rolling and mobility work: 10 minutes daily

Progressive Overload Strategies (Beyond Adding Weight):
- Increase reps within a range (8→10→12, then increase weight and restart at 8)
- Increase sets (add 1 set per exercise over a mesocycle)
- Improve form quality (more control, slower eccentrics)
- Reduce rest periods slightly
- Increase range of motion

Common Mistakes for 40+ Lifters:
1. Training like they're 25 (too heavy, too often, poor form)
2. Ignoring warm-up and mobility
3. Ego lifting — chasing PRs instead of chasing good form and progressive overload
4. Not deloading — accumulated fatigue leads to injuries, not gains
5. Neglecting posterior chain (focusing on bench press and bicep curls instead of rows and deadlifts)
6. Training through pain — "no pain no gain" mentality causes injuries that set back months of progress
